${\huge{Logistic}{~}{Regression}{~}{Modeling}{~}{-}{~}{Grad}{~}{School}{~}{Admission}}$

Multiple linear regression can be modified to model categorical or a count response variable (one type of classification modeling). Given a set of predictors, logistic regression can provide an estimate of the probability of a certain categorical response. There are three types of logistic regression, depending on the nature of the categorial response.

Binary Logistic Regression (the type of regression addressed in the work below) focuses on the probaility of an event occurring (respresented as "yes = 1") or an event not occurring (represented as "no = 0"). The example below will look at the probability of getting into graduate school (Admitted = 1 or Not Admitted = 0) based on grade-point-average (GPA) score, gradute-record-examination score (GRE) and the ranking of the candidate's undergraduate school.

Nominal Logistic Regression is applied when there are three or more response categories possible and there is no order to the response categories. Examples include, vehicle preferred (car, truck, SUV) and subjects picked (math, latin, biology).

Ordinal Logistic Regression is applied when there are three or more response categories and the responses are order ranked. It should be noted that the intervals between rankings do not have to be equal. Choice of levels of hot sauce is an example of an ordinal response.

Learning systems usually assume that the dataset used for learning is balanced (for binary logistic regression, 50% yes's - 50% no's). In the real world there are many examples where the dataset is imbalanced; examples include, cancer diagnosis and telephone fraud detection. For an imbalanced dataset the smaller percentage representing one of the two possible responses is called the minority class; the larger percentage class is called the majority class. A number of different strategies have been developed in order to overcome this class imbalance problem. One of these strategies is called over sampling and Synthetic Minority Over-sampling Technique (SMOTE) has been proven to work well in resolving the class imbalance issue.

${\huge{Binary}{~}{Logistic}{~}{Regression}{~}{Model}}$

The binary logistic regression model can be represented algebraically as follows.

$${\Large {logit(p)=log{\frac{p}{1 - p}}}={b_0{~}+{~}b_1x_1{~}...{~}+{~}b_{k}x_{k}}} $$

The above model states that the $\underline{natural}$ logrithm of the odds is a linear function of the ${x}$ variables and is called the "log odds". The left-side of the equation is known as the logit transformation of the probability of success, ${p}$.

Manipulating the above equation results in an equation for calculating ${p}$.

$${\Large {p={\frac{exp(b_0{~}+{~}b_1x_1{~}...{~}+{~}b_{k}x_{k})}{1+exp(b_0{~}+{~}b_1x_1{~}...{~}+{~}b_{k}x_{k})}}}} $$

The equations above provide a way of switching between odds and probability, but first a review of odds is in order.

As an example, say the probability of an event is 0.8, then the probability of a non-event will be 1 - 0.8 or 0.2. The odds of success (an event occurring) are defined as the probability of success over the probability of failure, in this case, 0.8/0.2 = 4. Putting it another way, the odds of success are 4 to 1 or a 300% increase in success over failure.

It is important to remember that while probability is limited from 0 to 1, odds, on the other hand, are limited from 0 to infinity, hence the need to tranform odds using the natural log function. For example, a probability of 0.999 is equivalent to odds of 999 and log odds of 6.907. This is important to understand and is related directly to the coefficients of the predictor variables developed by the logistic regression model.

As shown above, the model's "Classification Accuracy" or simply, "Accuracy", is 68%. This means that out of all the predictions made, the model is correct 68% of the time.

The "Precision" of the model indicates how often the model is correct when predicting a positive result or, in this example, predicting being admitted into grad school. Using this definition, the model is only correct 62% of the time. In other words, the model may indicate that a student is going to be admitted into grad school, but that is correct only 62% of the time.

For the students in the test dataset that have actually been admitted into grad school, the model can only identify them 23% of the time. This is known as "Recall", which is also known as "Sensitivity" or "True Positive Rate".

"Specificity", which is not shown here, indicates the ability of the model to predict false event or, in this case rejected event, when the student has actually been rejected. Using this definition, the model in this example is correct 92% of the time (60/(60 + 5) * 100).

The Receiver Operator Characteristic Curve (see below) plots true positive rate against false positive rate. The area under the curve (AUC) represents how closely the model predicts the admitted/rejected outcome or, put another way, the probability that the model will rank a randomly chosen positive instance higher than a randomly negative instance. An AUC score of 1 would indicate a perfect predictor, i.e. there is no overlap between true positives and false positives. An AUC score of 0.5, shown as the area under the red diagonal line, indicates that the model did not provide any useful information, similar to random guesses or flipping a coin. This means that the closer the ROC line is to the top left corner of the plot, the better the accuracy of the model.

The coefficients of the predictor variables require some interpretation.

For example, the GPA coefficient of 0.8040 reveals that, while holding the values of the other predictors fixed, for every unit increase in GPA score the odds of gaining admission to grad school $\underline{increase}$ by 123% (exp(0.8040) = 2.23; odds of 2.23 to 1). Or, if preferred, a probability of 0.69 (where, p = exp(0.8040)/(1 + exp(0.8040))).

Analysis of the ranking coefficients requires a different approach from that used for GRE and GPA. Here rankings are compared to a baseline ranking, in this case, the ranking of the top undergraduate school, which has a coefficient of -3.9900. Graduating from a second ranked undergraduate school, as compared to a top ranked undergraduate school, results in the odds of admission $\underline{decreasing}$ by approximately 49% (exp(-4.6654 - (-3.9900)) - 1 = -0.491). Or, if preferred, a probability of 0.34 (where, p = exp(-0.6754)/(1 + exp(-0.6754))) as compared to a top ranked undergraduate school.
